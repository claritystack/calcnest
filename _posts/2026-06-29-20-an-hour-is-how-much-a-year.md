  <h1>{{ page.title }}</h1>

  <p>Someone tells you a job pays $20 an hour, and your brain immediately tries to turn that into something more useful — a yearly number, a monthly number, something you can actually compare to bills and rent.</p>

  <p>Here's the full breakdown, gross and after taxes.</p>

  <h2>The quick number</h2>

  <p>Working full-time at 40 hours a week for 52 weeks, $20 an hour comes out to $41,600 a year.</p>

  <p>That's $800 a week, or about $3,467 a month, before anything gets taken out.</p>

  <h2>Why "before anything gets taken out" matters</h2>

  <p>That $41,600 is your gross pay — the number on your offer letter, not the number in your bank account.</p>

  <p>Once federal tax, FICA, and (depending on where you live) state tax come out, you're looking at meaningfully less. How much less depends almost entirely on your state.</p>

  <h2>What it looks like after taxes</h2>

  <p>For a single filer with no dependents, taking the standard deduction, federal tax plus FICA alone brings $41,600 down to somewhere around $35,000 to $36,000 a year.</p>

  <p>That's before state tax even enters the picture.</p>

  <h2>If you live somewhere with no state income tax</h2>

  <p>Texas, Florida, Nevada, Washington, and a few others don't touch your wages at all. If you're in one of these, that $35,000-ish federal-and-FICA number is close to your real take-home.</p>

  <p>Per paycheck, paid biweekly, that lands around $1,330 to $1,380.</p>

  <h2>If you live somewhere with state income tax</h2>

  <p>Most states take another bite, usually somewhere between 3% and 10% depending on where you live and how their brackets work.</p>

  <p>In a higher-tax state like California or New York, your real take-home on $20/hour often lands closer to $31,000 to $33,000 a year — noticeably below the no-tax-state number.</p>

  <h2>Per paycheck, broken down by schedule</h2>

  <p>The schedule your employer uses changes the math slightly, even though the yearly total stays the same.</p>

  <p>Weekly: $800 gross, before taxes.</p>

  <p>Biweekly: $1,600 gross, before taxes.</p>

  <p>Semi-monthly: roughly $1,733 gross, before taxes.</p>

  <p>Monthly: $3,467 gross, before taxes.</p>

  <p>After taxes, each of these drops by 15-25%, give or take, depending on your state and filing status.</p>

  <h2>What if you don't work full 52 weeks?</h2>

  <p>Two weeks of unpaid time off knocks about $1,600 off your yearly total, since that's two weeks at $800 each.</p>

  <p>Most full-time jobs offer some paid time off, which keeps you closer to the full $41,600 — but it's worth checking how your specific job handles this before assuming the full number applies.</p>

  <h2>Is $20 an hour actually a good wage?</h2>

  <p>It's above federal minimum wage by a wide margin, and above most state minimums too.</p>

  <p>Where it lands on "comfortable" versus "tight" depends heavily on cost of living. In a lower-cost area, $20/hour supports a fairly stable independent life. In an expensive metro — San Francisco, New York City, parts of California generally — the same wage stretches a lot less far.</p>

  </ul>

  <h2>A few more questions people usually have</h2>

  <h3>Is $20 an hour enough to live on?</h3>

  <p>It depends almost entirely on location. In lower cost-of-living areas, it's a workable, independent wage. In expensive cities, it gets tight fast, especially with rent factored in.</p>

  <h3>How much is $20 an hour biweekly after taxes?</h3>

  <p>Gross biweekly pay is $1,600. After federal tax and FICA, expect somewhere around $1,300 to $1,380, with state tax pulling that lower depending on where you live.</p>

  <h3>How much is $20 an hour monthly?</h3>

  <p>Gross monthly pay is about $3,467. After taxes, that typically lands somewhere between $2,600 and $3,000, depending on state and filing status.</p>

  <h3>What salary is equivalent to $20 an hour?</h3>

  <p>At a standard 40-hour week, $20/hour equals a $41,600 annual salary — that's the direct conversion employers use when comparing hourly roles to salaried ones.</p>
