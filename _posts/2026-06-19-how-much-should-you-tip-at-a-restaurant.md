  <h1>{{ page.title }}</h1>

  <p>The check arrives, the little payment screen flips around, and suddenly you're staring at three percentage options wondering which one actually makes sense. It's a strange moment, if you think about it — a small, quiet negotiation that happens at the end of nearly every meal out, one most people navigate on autopilot without ever really stopping to ask whether they're doing it right. The truth is, restaurant tipping in the US follows a fairly consistent set of norms once you understand the logic behind them, even though it can feel arbitrary in the moment.</p>

  <p>Let's start with the number everyone actually wants: somewhere between <strong>15% and 20%</strong> of the bill, before tax, is the range most servers and most diners would agree is fair for a typical sit-down meal. But that range hides a lot of nuance, and the right number for you on any given night depends on a handful of factors — the type of restaurant, the quality of service, the size of your group, and even which part of the country you're in. We'll walk through all of it.</p>

  <h2>Why 15–20% Became the Norm</h2>
  <p>Tipping in American restaurants isn't just a polite gesture — it's structurally built into how servers get paid. Federal law allows employers to pay tipped workers a direct cash wage as low as $2.13 an hour, with the expectation that tips will make up the difference to at least the standard minimum wage. In many states, this gap is even more pronounced, since some states allow the full federal tipped minimum while others require employers to pay a higher base. Either way, the practical result is the same: for a huge number of servers, tips aren't a bonus on top of a living wage, they essentially *are* the wage.</p>

  <p>That context matters because it explains why under-tipping carries more social weight in the US than in countries where servers are paid a full standard wage regardless of tips. A 20% tip in Tokyo would be considered unusual, even slightly confusing. A 10% tip in New York, depending on the situation, might come across as a quiet signal that something went wrong.</p>

  <p>Over the last decade, the baseline itself has crept upward. Older etiquette guides will tell you 15% is standard — and technically, it's still within the acceptable range for decent service — but in most cities today, 18% to 20% functions as the real default, especially with digital payment terminals nudging customers toward higher suggested percentages (you've probably noticed the screen rarely offers you a "10%" button anymore).</p>

  <h2>Does the Restaurant Type Change the Math?</h2>
  <p>It does, and this is one of the more common points of confusion. The 15–20% range applies most cleanly to standard, full-service sit-down restaurants — the kind where a server takes your order, brings your food, checks in periodically, and handles your payment. This covers the vast majority of casual dining experiences, from neighborhood diners to mid-range chains.</p>

  <p>Fine dining shifts the expectation upward. The level of service is more involved — wine pairing suggestions, carefully timed courses, a server who's genuinely attentive without hovering — and 20% or more is the norm here. Some upscale restaurants build a service charge directly into the bill, so it's worth scanning for that line item before adding anything extra on top.</p>

  <p>Counter-service and fast-casual spots sit at the opposite end. You're ordering at a register, often carrying your own food to the table, with minimal direct service involved. Tipping here is genuinely optional — a dollar or two if you'd like, nothing if you'd rather not. The rise of tip prompts on every payment screen, even at coffee shops and sandwich counters, has created some social pressure that didn't exist a decade ago, but the underlying etiquette hasn't actually changed: optional service deserves an optional tip, not a guilt-driven 20%.</p>

  <h2>Pre-Tax or Post-Tax?</h2>
  <p>Most people calculate their tip based on the subtotal before sales tax is applied, and this is generally considered the more "correct" method, since the tax has nothing to do with the service itself. That said, the difference between tipping pre-tax versus post-tax is usually small — a matter of cents on most checks, maybe a dollar or two on a larger one in a high-tax state like California or New York. If you'd rather just tip on the total to keep the math simple, nobody is going to take issue with the extra generosity.</p>

  <h2>When the Service Was Genuinely Bad</h2>
  <p>This is where things get a little more personal, and where most of the hesitation at the table actually comes from. If your server was inattentive, dismissive, or made repeated mistakes that were clearly within their control, dropping your tip to around 10% is a reasonable way to register that the experience fell short — without going all the way to zero, which tends to be read as an extreme statement reserved for truly outrageous situations.</p>

  <p>It's worth pausing, though, to separate a server's performance from the kitchen's. If your food came out cold, wrong, or absurdly late, that's very often outside your server's control entirely — they didn't cook it, and in a lot of restaurants they don't even see it until it's already plated. Punishing the tip for a kitchen mistake ends up penalizing the wrong person. The better move in that case is to tip normally and mention the issue to a manager directly, which actually has a chance of fixing the problem for next time.</p>

  <h2>Large Groups and Automatic Gratuity</h2>
  <p>If you're dining with a group of six or more, don't be surprised to find an automatic gratuity — usually 18% to 20% — already added to the bill. Restaurants do this because large tables are genuinely more work to coordinate, and individual tipping in big groups tends to be inconsistent at best. The one real risk here is accidentally double-tipping: scan your bill carefully before adding anything extra, since this is one of the most common (and easily avoidable) mistakes in group dining.</p>

  <h2>Splitting the Tip Fairly</h2>
  <p>When a group is splitting the bill and orders varied wildly in price, the cleanest approach is for each person to apply the same tip percentage to their own portion of the subtotal, rather than trying to land on one average number across everyone. It keeps things proportional and avoids the awkward moment where someone who ordered a salad ends up subsidizing someone else's steak and three cocktails.</p>

  <h2>Frequently Asked Questions(FAQs)</h2>

  <h3>1) Is a 10% tip disrespectful?</h3>
  <p>Not inherently — context matters. As a deliberate response to genuinely poor, server-controlled service, 10% reads as a clear but measured signal rather than an insult. The problem is when 10% becomes someone's default for *normal* service, since that falls noticeably below the standard 15–20% range and can come across as a discount on effort that was actually fine.</p>

  <h3>2) What is the 20% tip trick?</h3>
  <p>It's a quick mental math shortcut, not a formal rule. Move the decimal point one place to the left to get 10% of the bill, then double that number to land on 20%. On a $48 bill, 10% is $4.80, so 20% is $9.60. It's popular precisely because it removes the need for a calculator or phone at the table — though plenty of people just use ours instead.</p>

  <h3>3) Is it rude not to tip at all?</h3>
  <p>In the context of full-service, sit-down dining in the US, yes — leaving zero is widely viewed as a significant breach of etiquette, given how directly server pay depends on tips. It's a different conversation at counter-service spots, where tipping is optional to begin with and skipping it carries no real social weight.</p>

  <h3>4) Is 20% considered a generous tip?</h3>
  <p>It's generous relative to the older 15% baseline, but in many cities today it's closer to the expected norm for good service rather than an exceptional gesture. If you want to signal genuinely outstanding service, 22–25% is where that distinction starts to show. 20% has simply become the new "solid, no complaints" tip rather than a standout one.</p>

  <h3>5) Should you tip more for takeout?</h3>
  <p>Tipping on takeout orders is optional and generally lighter than sit-down service — $1 to $2, or around 10% for larger or more complex orders, is reasonable. Someone still assembled and packaged your order, even if no one waited on your table.</p>
