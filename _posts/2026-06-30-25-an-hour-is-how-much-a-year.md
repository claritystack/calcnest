  <h1>{{ page.title }}</h1>

  <p>$25 an hour comes up a lot — it's a common starting point for skilled trades, entry-level tech support, healthcare assistant roles, and plenty of jobs that sit just above minimum wage but below what most people think of as a "good salary."</p>

  <p>So what does it actually come out to once you stop thinking in hourly terms?</p>

  <h2>The straightforward math</h2>

  <p>At 40 hours a week, every week of the year, $25/hour works out to $52,000 annually.</p>

  <p>It's a clean number, which is part of why $25/hour gets used so often in salary comparisons — multiply by 2,080 (the standard full-time hours in a year) and you land right on $52,000.</p>

  <h2>How that compares to the median US wage</h2>

  <p>$52,000 a year sits close to, and in many years slightly above, the median individual income in the US. It's not a high earner's salary, but it's solidly in the middle of the pack nationally — though "middle" looks very different depending on whether you're in rural Ohio or downtown Seattle.</p>

  <h2>Gross pay by pay period</h2>

  <p>Weekly: $1,000.</p>

  <p>Biweekly: $2,000.</p>

  <p>Semi-monthly: about $2,167.</p>

  <p>Monthly: about $4,333.</p>

  <p>All of these are gross — before federal tax, state tax, or FICA touches any of it.</p>

  <h2>What actually lands in your account</h2>

  <p>Federal tax and FICA alone typically bring $52,000 down to roughly $43,000 to $44,500 for a single filer, depending on exactly how the brackets shake out with the standard deduction applied.</p>

  <p>State tax is where things branch off significantly from there.</p>

  <h2>No state income tax</h2>

  <p>States like Texas, Florida, and Nevada don't add anything on top, so your $43,000-ish federal-and-FICA number is close to your final take-home.</p>

  <p>That puts your real biweekly paycheck somewhere around $1,650 to $1,700.</p>

  <h2>With state income tax</h2>

  <p>In a state like California, with its higher progressive brackets, take-home on $52,000 often falls closer to $38,000 to $40,000 for the year.</p>

  <p>New York lands somewhere similar, though slightly less aggressive than California at this income level. The gap between a no-tax state and a high-tax state on this salary can easily run $3,000–$5,000 a year — a real difference, not a rounding error.</p>

  <h2>Does $25/hour qualify you for anything specific?</h2>

  <p>It's a common threshold mentioned for apartment rental income requirements (many landlords want gross income at roughly 3x monthly rent), auto loan approvals, and some mortgage pre-qualification ranges — though every lender and landlord sets their own bar.</p>

  <p>It's also above the wage floor for overtime eligibility under federal law in most circumstances, meaning time-and-a-half typically applies if you work beyond 40 hours in a week.</p>

  <h2>If you're comparing job offers</h2>

  <p>$25/hour and a $52,000 salary aren't automatically identical in practice, even though the math says they are.</p>

  <p>Hourly roles sometimes come with overtime opportunity that salaried roles don't, while salaried roles sometimes come with benefits (health insurance, 401k matching, PTO) that tip the real value higher than the hourly number alone suggests. Worth weighing both sides, not just the headline number.</p>

  <h2>Frequently Asked Questions(FAQs)</h2>

  <h3>1) Is $25 an hour a livable wage?</h3>

  <p>In most of the country, yes, particularly outside of major coastal cities. It's enough to cover rent, basic expenses, and some savings in moderate cost-of-living areas, though it gets considerably tighter in expensive metros.</p>

  <h3>2) How much is $25 an hour after taxes per paycheck?</h3>

  <p>Paid biweekly, expect roughly $1,500 to $1,700 after federal tax and FICA, with state tax pulling the number down further depending on where you live.</p>

  <h3>3) Is $52,000 a year considered middle class?</h3>

  <p>By most national income measures, yes — it falls within typical middle-class income ranges, though "middle class" varies significantly by region and household size.</p>

  <h3>4) How many hours a week is $25 an hour to make $1,000?</h3>

  <p>Exactly 40 hours at $25/hour comes out to $1,000 before taxes, which is the standard full-time week most of these numbers assume.</p>
